Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is a molecule that contains the genetic instructions used in the development and function of all living organisms. It is made up of two strands of nucleotides that are twisted together in a double helix structure. The sequence of nucleotides in DNA determines the genetic information encoded in the molecule, which is used to synthesize proteins and other essential molecules in the cell.

Nucleotides are the basic building blocks of DNA and RNA. They consist of a sugar molecule, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. For DNA, a nucleotide could either A, C, G, or T. The T is substituted with U in RNA

A Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms(SNP) is a genetic variation that occurs when a single nucleotide (A, C, G, or T) is altered in a DNA sequence. This variation can occur in both coding and non-coding regions of the genome and can affect how genes are expressed or function. SNPs are the most common type of genetic variation and can be used to study genetic diseases, predict disease risk, and tailor medical treatment to an individual's genetic profile.

Codons are sequences of three nucleotides that code for specific amino acids. E.g, ACG codes for Threonine.
